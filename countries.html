Australia

Australia offers excellent opportunities for international students, both in terms of education and the potential to stay permanently. While the journey requires careful planning—such as choosing the right course, meeting visa requirements, and eventually seeking pathways to permanent residency—many students find that Australia’s quality of life, educational institutions, and career prospects make it a top choice for international study and migration.

Studying and living permanently in Australia as an international student is an exciting opportunity, but it requires understanding the various steps, requirements, and opportunities available. Here's a detailed breakdown:

1. Study in Australia: Visa Requirements

  • Student Visa (subclass 500): This is the primary visa for international students. You must apply for this visa before arriving in Australia and can study full-time at an accredited institution.
  • Eligibility:
  • You need to be enrolled in a full-time course in Australia (degree, diploma, or certificate).
  • Meet English language proficiency requirements (IELTS, TOEFL, or other recognized tests).
  • Provide Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) during your stay.

2. Cost of Studying and Living

  • Tuition Fees: Depending on the course and university, international students may pay between AUD 20,000 to AUD 45,000 per year for undergraduate programs, and AUD 22,000 to AUD 50,000 for postgraduate programs.
  • Living Cost:
  • Accommodation: AUD 440–1,500 per month depending on location.
  • Food: AUD 200–300 per month.
  • Transport: AUD 50–200 per month, depending on the city and whether you use public transport.
  • Personal expenses: AUD 100–200 per month.

3. Popular Study Destinations in Australia

  • Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, and Adelaide are some of the top cities for international students, with a high concentration of world-renowned universities and a diverse student community.
  • Top Universities:
  • University of Melbourne
  • Australian National University (ANU)
  • University of Sydney
  • University of Queensland
  • Monash University
  • University of New South Wales (UNSW)

4. Settlement and Life in Australia

  • Cultural Diversity: Australia is home to a highly diverse population, and international students often feel welcome due to the multicultural environment. Many cities have student clubs and international student associations to help you adjust to life in Australia.
  • Healthcare: As an international student, you must have Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) for the duration of your stay in Australia. OSHC covers basic medical expenses and emergencies. After becoming a permanent resident, you can access Medicare, Australia’s public healthcare system.
  • Job Opportunities: The job market is competitive, especially in cities like Sydney and Melbourne. However, there are plenty of opportunities for students in industries like hospitality, retail, and technology. Graduates in specialized fields (e.g., engineering, IT, healthcare) have more options to secure employment.

United Kingdom

Studying and living permanently in the UK as an international student involves understanding visa requirements, costs, post-study work opportunities, and pathways to permanent residency. Here's a detailed breakdown of what international students need to know about studying in the UK and the routes to permanent settlement.

The UK offers a broad range of opportunities for international students, from prestigious universities to pathways for permanent residency. Although the journey to permanent settlement may take time, with routes like the Graduate Visa, Skilled Worker Visa, and eventually ILR, international students have various pathways to make the UK their long-term home. Whether you're looking to gain work experience after graduation or pursue a career in the UK, the country has many advantages for those who wish to study and live there permanently.

1. Study in the UK: Visa Requirements

  • Student Visa (previously Tier 4 Visa): This is the primary visa for international students wishing to study in the UK. You must apply for this visa before arriving in the UK and have an offer from a licensed educational institution.
  • Eligibility:
  • You need to be enrolled in a full-time course (degree, diploma, or certificate) at a recognized institution.
  • You need to meet the English language proficiency requirements (usually via IELTS, TOEFL, or equivalent tests).
  • You will need to provide proof of accommodation and health insurance.
  • Working Rights: International students on a student visa can work up to 20 hours per week during term time and full-time during vacations. If you're studying at a higher education level (postgraduate or degree level), you may be allowed to work full-time during the holidays.

2. Cost of Studying and Living

  • Tuition Fees: The cost of tuition varies depending on the course and the university. On average, international students pay:
  • Undergraduate courses: £10,000–£38,000 per year, with medical and veterinary courses being at the higher end.
  • Postgraduate courses: £12,000–£35,000 per year.
  • Living Cost:
  • Accommodation: £400–£1,200 per month, depending on the location and type of accommodation (on-campus or private).
  • Food: £150–£250 per month.
  • Transport: £50–£150 per month, depending on the city.
  • Miscellaneous: £150–£200 per month for personal expenses.
  • Scholarships: Many UK universities offer scholarships for international students, which can significantly reduce tuition costs. Some government programs also provide funding options, such as the Chevening Scholarships or Commonwealth Scholarships.

3. Top UK Universities for International Students

  • University of Oxford
  • University of Cambridge
  • Imperial College London
  • London School of Economics and Political Science (LSE)
  • University College London (UCL)
  • University of Edinburgh
  • University of Manchester
  • King’s College London

4. Living in the UK: Culture and Lifestyle

  • Cultural Diversity: The UK is known for its rich cultural diversity, and international students can experience this in cities like London, Manchester, Birmingham, and Edinburgh. The UK is home to students from all around the world, creating a vibrant and inclusive environment.
  • Healthcare: International students are eligible for the National Health Service (NHS) if they are on a student visa. You need to pay the Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) as part of your visa application, which covers most medical treatment.
  • Public Transport: Public transportation in the UK is widely accessible, with buses, trains, and the London Underground (Tube) being common. You can use student discount cards, like the 16-25 Railcard, to reduce travel costs.

Canada

Studying and living in Canada as an international student is a popular option due to its high-quality education system, multicultural society, and excellent post-study work and permanent residency pathways. Here's a detailed guide on how international students can study and eventually settle in Canada.

Canada provides a clear and structured pathway for international students to not only receive world-class education but also transition to permanent residency. With various immigration programs like Express Entry, Provincial Nominee Programs, and Post-Graduation Work Permits, international students have the chance to build their careers in Canada and settle permanently.


1. Study in Canada: Visa Requirements

  • Study Permit (Student Visa): International students need a study permit to study in Canada for more than 6 months. You need to apply for this permit before you travel to Canada.
  • Eligibility:
  • You must have a valid letter of acceptance from a Designated Learning Institution (DLI).
  • You must demonstrate sufficient funds to cover tuition fees, living expenses, and return travel (usually around CAD 10,000 per year for living expenses, excluding tuition).
  • You must prove that you intend to leave Canada when your study permit expires.
  • If your course is longer than 6 months, you need to apply for a study permit. For shorter programs, you can study without one but will need a visitor visa.
  • Working Rights: As an international student, you are allowed to work up to 20 hours per week during term time and full-time during breaks (summer, winter holidays, etc.). If your program is at a recognized institution, you can also work off-campus without a work permit.

2. Cost of Studying and Living

  • Tuition Fees: The cost of tuition for international students varies greatly by program and institution, typically ranging from CAD 7,000 to CAD 29,000 per year.
  • Living Costs:
  • Accommodation: CAD 500–1,500 per month depending on the location.
  • Food: CAD 300–500 per month.
  • Transportation: CAD 80–150 per month depending on the city.
  • Miscellaneous: CAD 100–200 per month for personal expenses.

3. Top Canadian Universities

  • University of Toronto
  • University of British Columbia (UBC)
  • McGill University
  • University of Alberta
  • McMaster University
  • University of Montreal
  • University of Ottawa
  • Queen’s University

4. Permanent Residency Pathways

  • Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP): After completing a degree or diploma program in Canada, you can apply for a work permit to gain valuable Canadian work experience, which can help you qualify for permanent residency.
  • Express Entry: If you have Canadian work experience, you may qualify for Express Entry, Canada's immigration system that processes skilled workers, tradespeople, and graduates for permanent residency.
  • Provincial Nominee Programs (PNP): Each province has its own immigration programs, and international students may be eligible to apply for provincial nomination after graduation.
